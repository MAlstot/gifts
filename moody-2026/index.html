Teach for Integrity: AI, the Eleven Reasons, and Your Course

Moody Online Programs Faculty Workshop · July 23, 2026 · Megan Alstot, Academy for Teaching & Learning, Baylor University

The foundation: why students misuse AI

Cheating has always existed — AI changed how, not why. Eleven research-backed reasons explain the why, and every tool on this page answers one or more of them.

  • The Eleven Reasons: A Mentoring Matrix — each reason with its guiding question, what feeds it, and the integrity builders that answer it (Bertram Gallant & Rettinger, 2025).

Six small teaching moves — your map to this page

Pick ONE to try this term. Each move is small on purpose, and its tool lives right here.

  1. Take the pulse. You can’t mentor students you haven’t met — and most misuse pressure is visible in week one, before the first assignment.
    → Learner Information Survey  (editable Word version)
  2. Name the kernel of cognition. No tool needed — one sentence, written at the top of your assignment: “The thinking this assignment exists to build is ___.” Every AI decision downstream gets easier, and Moves 3 and 4 will ask you for it.
  3. Place yourself on the continuum. Where an assignment sits decides which rails it needs. Two tools, in order — first understand the levels, then diagnose your own course.
    → The AI Literacy Continuum — how the five levels build, and what each protects cognitively
    → Continuum self-evaluation — a reflection tool: place your course now, set a target, keep it for later
  4. Stress test the assignment. Your students meet your assignment on their worst day — meet it there first. Seven personas built from the eleven reasons show you where it strains. It diagnoses; it does not fix — the reading of the diagnosis, and what to do about it, stays yours.
    → Assignment Integrity Stress Test — run it once, build a personalized version, or save it as a Copilot skill
  5. Redesign what needs it — with AI as your thinking partner. Now act on the diagnosis, on your terms. Design work is where AI is strongest and your kernel isn’t at stake. AI proposes; you decide.
    → Design Prompt Starters — four copy-ready prompts to scaffold, rethink, and strengthen a weak spot the stress test surfaced
  6. Develop & place your policy — last. Once the assignment is settled, write the rail. Silence reads as permission, so the rail — with its why — belongs where temptation happens: the assignment brief, not just the syllabus.
    → Guardrail Starters — copy-ready rails keyed to your continuum level, for all four Ds
A note on “No AI.” We’re talking about AI-free assignments, not AI-free courses. A blanket course-wide ban isn’t feasible or inclusive — it would rule out the library’s AI-powered search (Baylor’s OneSearch) and the assistive tools many students, including neurodivergent learners, rely on to access their education. The intentional move is naming which uses are off the table for a specific assignment, and being precise about what we each mean by “AI.”
