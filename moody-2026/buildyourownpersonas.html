Build Your Own Personas — Customization Template

The seven personas in the Assignment Integrity Stress Test are research-backed composites. But you already carry your own — the students who come to mind the moment you picture your class. Building the test around those makes it dramatically more honest, and you don't need any framework to start. Just the students you've actually taught.

Where to get the raw material: the students and behaviors already top of mind — the one who emails at 11:58, the one who apologizes before every question, the one who's brilliant in discussion and silent on the page. Add what your learner survey surfaced (confidence, time pressures, which AI tools they use), patterns from past semesters, and your discipline's pressure points (licensure exams, portfolio reviews, working-professional schedules).

Privacy note (Diligence): personas are composites, never real, identifiable students. No names, no identifying details, and never paste real student data — survey responses included — into an unprotected AI tool. Use Baylor's protected Copilot, and aggregate before you paste (e.g., "many of my students report low confidence in academic writing").

The persona recipe

Start from a student or a behavior you've seen. Three ingredients — fill in one card per persona:

PERSONA NAME: The ___________________
  (A memorable label for the behavior, not a real person)

BAD DAY: One or two sentences. What is true for this student the week
  your assignment is due? (Time, confidence, competing obligations,
  what they believe about the rules, what tools are open in the
  other tab.)

WHAT THEY SCAN FOR: What in the assignment will this student read
  closely — and what gap or ambiguity might they fall into? Be
  specific about how AI would enter the picture for them.

Worked example (graduate, online, working professionals)

PERSONA NAME: The Sunday-Night Director

BAD DAY: Full-time K-12 administrator in an online EdD. It's Sunday,
  9 PM, the discussion post is due at midnight, and a board meeting
  ate the weekend. Copilot is already open from work.

WHAT THEY SCAN FOR: Whether anyone will actually read this post
  closely. Whether the prompt could be answered without the readings.
  If the post only needs to "sound right," AI writes it in 40 seconds
  — and next week's post, too.

Prompts to help you draft (paste into your AI)

  • "I teach [discipline] [modality]. Here's a student behavior I've seen: [describe it in your own words]. Turn it into a persona card — give it a memorable name, a 'bad day,' and what they'd scan for in an assignment. Keep it a composite, not a real person."
  • "Here are 3 student behaviors I want to design for: [list them]. Draft a persona card for each, in the format name / bad day / what they scan for."

Optional: the eleven reasons as a deeper lens

You don't need the eleven reasons to build good personas — your observation is enough. But if you want to understand why a behavior shows up, or to rethink a puzzling behavior through a research lens, the eleven reasons are there for that: self-efficacy, expectations, values, motivation, procrastination, cost-benefit, moral disengagement, contract cheating, peer effects, narcissism, instructor effects. (See the Eleven Reasons matrix for what each means and the integrity builders that answer it.)

A behavior you've observed usually maps to one or two of them — and naming the reason can turn "this student cheated" into "this student was under a pressure I can design around." That reframe is the whole point; the mapping is a bonus, not a requirement.

Swapping personas into the stress test

In the stress-test prompt (or Skill), replace any of the seven default personas with yours, or add yours alongside them. There's no rule about covering every reason — use the students who actually pressure-test your assignment. Keep the three failure layers and the diagnose-then-hand-back flow unchanged: the personas are the lens; the machinery is the test.
