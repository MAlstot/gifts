Learner Information Survey — First-Week Question Bank

Small Teaching Move 1: Take the pulse. Run this in week one (Google Form, Canvas ungraded survey, or Qualtrics). Your students are professionals in careers — administrators, designers, leaders — so ask them like the adults they are. Ten minutes of their time tells you what each one is here to develop, where confidence is solid or shaky, which reasons for misuse are already in the room, and which AI tools your guardrails actually need to name. Keep it ungraded and say why you're asking: "This helps me teach you, not evaluate you."

Each block below is copy-ready. Cut what you don't need — a 12-question survey gets finished; a 40-question survey gets abandoned. Items are tagged with the reason(s) they surface, from Bertram Gallant & Rettinger (2025).

Block A — Who are you? (relationship · instructor effects)

  1. What name do you go by, and how do I pronounce it?
  2. What's happening in your life this term that could compete with this course? (Work, family, other courses — whatever you're comfortable sharing.)
  3. What are you here to develop? What do you want to gain from this program — as a scholar, as a practitioner, or both? (values · motivation — this answer is the anchor for every AI conversation you'll have with them)

Block B — Confidence (self-efficacy)

  1. How confident are you that you can succeed in the core work of this course (e.g., the writing / analysis / problem sets)? Very confident · Somewhat confident · Not very confident · I'm honestly worried
  2. Which part of the coursework feels furthest from your current strengths right now? (open response)
  3. When you get stuck on hard academic work, what do you usually do first? Push through alone · Ask a classmate · Ask the instructor · Search online · Ask an AI tool · Put it off (self-efficacy · procrastination — the "Ask an AI tool" and "Put it off" answers are your early-warning list)

Block C — Your AI habits (expectations · the guardrail targeting data)

  1. Which AI tools do you actually use? (Check all that apply) Microsoft Copilot · ChatGPT · Claude · Gemini · Grammarly · Other: ___ · I don't use AI tools
  2. If you checked any: which one do you use most, and do you know which version/plan (free or paid)? (This tells you which tool to stress-test your assignments in — guardrails behave differently in different tools and versions.)
  3. What do you mostly use AI for in your studies right now? (Check all that apply) Brainstorming ideas · Explaining concepts · Summarizing readings · Improving my grammar/clarity · Drafting text I then revise · Drafting text I mostly submit · Checking my work · Making study aids · I don't use it for school (The two "Drafting" options are deliberately adjacent — the gap between them is where your integrity conversation starts.)
  4. In your other courses, what's the AI rule? Mostly allowed · Mostly banned · Different in every course · Honestly, I'm never sure (expectations — expect "never sure" to win)

Block D — Where the lines are (expectations · moral disengagement)

  1. In THIS course, how clear are you right now on what AI use is allowed? Clear · Somewhat clear · Not clear yet
  2. True or false, in your honest opinion: "Using AI to write part of an assignment isn't really cheating if you edit it after." True · False · It depends — and I could explain on what (Don't grade this. It's a conversation-starter for your synchronous session — the "it depends" answers are gold.)
  3. What's one question about AI use in this course you'd like answered before the first big assignment? (open response)

Block E — Time and pressure (procrastination · cost-benefit)

  1. Realistically, when do you usually start big assignments? Right away · About halfway to the deadline · The final few days · The night before
  2. What would help you most when a deadline collides with the rest of your life? A short no-questions extension · Scaffolded checkpoints so I can't fall behind · A clear late policy · Talking to the instructor early (Their answers are a menu of integrity builders — pick one and actually offer it.)

Reading the results — three moves

Move A: Sort Block B. Anyone reporting low confidence gets an early, specific, low-stakes win and feedback before anything counts. Low self-efficacy is the most fixable of the eleven reasons — and in professionals, it usually hides behind competence everywhere else in their lives.

Move B: Tabulate Q7–Q8. That's your stress-test target list: when you run the Assignment Integrity Stress Test, test your guardrail language in the tool and version your students actually use — a rail that holds in Copilot may leak in the free tier of another tool.

Move C: Bring Q10–Q12 results (aggregated, anonymous) to your first AI conversation with the class. "Here's what we said" beats "here's my policy" — it makes the integrity conversation a mentoring move instead of a policing one.

Privacy (Diligence): survey responses are student data. Keep them in Baylor-protected tools; if you paste anything into an AI, aggregate first ("60% of students report…"), never individual responses.
