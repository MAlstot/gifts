# The Assignment Integrity Stress Test

*Adapted from Doan Winkel's "7 Bad-Day Students" lesson-plan-review skill, with student personas built from the eleven reasons in Tricia Bertram Gallant & David Rettinger's* The Opposite of Cheating *(2025), the Anthropic 4D AI Fluency Framework, and the AI Literacy Continuum. Assembled by Megan Alstot, Academy for Teaching & Learning, Baylor University.*

Most instructors review an assignment as the instructor. You know the intent, the learning it builds, and the rules you assume are obvious.

Your students experience your assignment as a system — at 11 PM, with three other deadlines, low confidence, an ambiguous AI policy, and Copilot already open in another tab. If the system relies on motivation, confidence, clear-to-you-only rules, or "they know better," it breaks.

Cheating has always existed. AI did not change *why* students cheat — it changed *how*. This skill stress-tests your assignment against the *why*.

---

## The core job

Take an assignment the user provides and stress-test it through 7 student personas grounded in the eleven reasons students cheat.

Find every integrity failure point. Fix them with integrity builders and guardrails matched to the assignment's declared AI literacy level. Re-test until clean.

**Output format:** Misuse-Pressure Report (what breaks and why) + rebuilt assignment that passes all 7 students, including student-facing guardrail language and the *why* behind each rail.

---

## Before starting: gather context

**STOP. Do not run the personas until you have the assignment. If the user has not pasted it yet, ask them to paste it now.**

You need these inputs:

1. **The assignment** — the actual prompt/brief students see. Rough draft is fine.
2. **The kernel of cognition** — one sentence: "The thinking this assignment exists to build is ___." (What must the student's own mind do for the learning to happen?)
3. **AI literacy continuum level** — where does this assignment sit?
   - **Level 1 · No AI Use** (a deliberate pedagogical choice, not a default)
   - **Level 2 · Limited AI Use** (specific, narrow tasks only)
   - **Level 3 · Collaborative AI Use** (student drafts, AI critiques; student keeps the pen)
   - **Level 4 · Integrated AI Use** (AI as professional tool inside the workflow)
   - **Level 5 · AI Innovation** (students design novel AI uses with self-set rails)
4. **Discipline, course level, and modality** (online/sync/async matters for what "visible process" can look like)
5. **What you know about your students** — if you've run a learner information survey, share what it surfaced: confidence levels, anxiety, which AI platforms they actually use.

Defaults if unspecified: graduate-level online course, Level 2, mixed student confidence.

## Step 0: A ruthless structural audit

Before personas, run a fast reality check. Answer PASS/FAIL in bullets:

- **The "why" is visible.** Students can see what this assignment builds and why it matters to their goals — not just what to submit.
- **The AI expectation is explicit for THIS assignment.** Allowed uses, forbidden uses, and the reason for the line. "Don't use AI inappropriately" is not a line — it's an invitation to guess.
- **The kernel is protected by design.** The thinking the assignment exists to build cannot be silently offloaded without the artifact changing visibly.
- **Process is visible, not just product.** There are artifacts along the way (notes, outline, draft, prompt log, reflection) — authorship has a trail.
- **The time math works.** Deadlines are scaffolded; a student who starts late still has a legitimate path other than surrender to AI.
- **Stakes are distributed.** No single high-stakes cliff that makes the cost-benefit calculation rational.
- **The relationship exists.** Somewhere in the arc, the instructor sees or responds to each student's developing work. Students do not cheat people they know.

If any fail, log them as failures to fix during the rebuild.

---

## Step 1: Spawn the 7 personas

Each persona is a real student on a bad day, reading your assignment as a system. Together they carry all eleven reasons.

**1. The Doubter** *(Self-Efficacy)*
Bad day: convinced they can't do this at the level required. AI feels less like cheating and more like survival. Scans the assignment for evidence they'll get feedback and practice before stakes rise. If confidence has no runway, they hand the task to AI.

**2. The Line-Guesser** *(Expectations)*
Bad day: genuinely unsure what counts as cheating here. Last course allowed AI brainstorming; this one says nothing. Reads silence and ambiguity as permission. If allowed/forbidden uses aren't named *for this assignment*, they guess — and guess generously.

**3. The Efficiency Rationalizer** *(Values + Moral Disengagement)*
Bad day: sees busywork. Can't find the "why," has no room for their own voice or goals, and has a rationalization ready: "everyone does it," "it doesn't hurt anyone," "this is just a hoop." If the assignment's purpose is invisible, AI use feels like efficiency, not a violation.

**4. The 11 PM Procrastinator** *(Procrastination + Cost-Benefit)*
Bad day: the deadline is tonight, and a full work week plus family obligations ate the runway — these are professionals with careers, and the deadline never asked. Time pressure has narrowed the options to one. Calculates: upside high, detection risk low, relationship with instructor thin. If there's a single end-of-arc deadline and no grace path, AI is the only door still open.

**5. The Ghost-Writer Shopper** *(Contract Cheating)*
Bad day: treats AI as an author-for-hire. Will submit whatever the tool produces, wholesale, if only the final product is ever seen. If authorship is undefined and process is invisible, the line between AI-as-tool and AI-as-author disappears.

**6. The Norm Reader** *(Peer Effects + Instructor Effects)*
Bad day: takes cues from the room. Classmates openly use AI on everything; the instructor doesn't seem to know anyone's name or read anyone's work closely. If the class culture is anonymous and the instructor seems checked out, "everyone does it" becomes true.

**7. The Grade Maximizer** *(Motivation + Narcissism)*
Bad day: plays for the grade, not the learning — and privately suspects rules are for other people. Rewarded by any design that grades end product only. If there's no structural cost for shortcuts (checkpoints, presence, process), they take the shortcut every time.

---

## Step 2: Run the 3 failure layers

Each of the 7 personas answers ALL 3 questions about the assignment. No skipping. Point to the exact sentence, gap, or design choice that breaks.

**Layer 1 · TEMPTATION & AMBIGUITY (reading the assignment):**
"Reading this, I would consider misusing AI because ___."
Common causes: invisible purpose, unstated or vague AI policy, no confidence runway, one high-stakes cliff, no student choice or voice.

**Layer 2 · KERNEL SURRENDER (doing the work):**
"While working, AI would end up doing the thinking because ___."
Common causes: the kernel (the thinking the assignment builds) can be offloaded without visible change; tasks worded as product requests ("write a paper on...") rather than thinking demands; no checkpoints where developing thought is seen; no draft-first structure.

**Layer 3 · EVIDENCE & ACCOUNTABILITY (submitting):**
"I could submit AI's work with no trace because ___."
Common causes: product-only grading; no process artifacts; no reflection or defense of choices; authorship and disclosure undefined; nothing connects the submission to the student's developing body of work.

This produces 21 failure checks per assignment (7 personas × 3 layers).

---

## Step 3: Tally and prioritize

| Severity | Threshold | Action |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| **CRITICAL** | 3+ personas flagged the same failure | Must be fixed. No exceptions. |
| **HIGH PRIORITY** | 2 personas flagged the same failure | Should be fixed. |
| **LOW** | 1 persona flagged it | Fix if it doesn't break something else. |

Group failures by where they hit: purpose & framing · AI expectations & guardrails · confidence & stakes structure · process visibility & checkpoints · evidence, reflection & disclosure.

---

## Step 4: Rebuild

For every CRITICAL and HIGH PRIORITY failure:

1. **State the failure.** Which personas flagged it and what broke.
2. **State the fix.** Which integrity builder answers it, and why.
3. **Show the rewrite.** The rebuilt section, in student-facing language.

### Rebuild rules

- **Mentor, don't police.** Every fix should read as design and invitation, not surveillance and threat. Detection tools are not fixes.
- **Match guardrails to the declared continuum level.** A Level 1 (No AI) assignment gets AI-resistant design + the *why* stated openly; a Level 3 gets draft-first structure with "AI critiques, never writes" rails; a Level 5 gets student-authored rails with justification. Never write a rail that demands a higher (or lower) literacy level than the assignment declares.
- **Every rail ships with its why.** One student-facing sentence: what this rail protects and how it serves their goals. Rails without reasons read as arbitrary — and arbitrary rules feed moral disengagement.
- **Protect the kernel structurally.** If the thinking can be offloaded invisibly, add an artifact that makes the thinking visible (annotated sources, decision memo, prompt log, viva/voice note, in-class connection).
- **Fix the time math.** Scaffolded deadlines (outline → draft → final), grace policies, and low-stakes checkpoints beat exhortation every time.
- **When fixes conflict, prioritize: Temptation & ambiguity > Kernel surrender > Evidence & accountability.** A student who never feels the pull never needs the audit trail.

### Standard components to add when needed

**A. The AI expectations block (for the assignment brief)** — allowed uses · forbidden uses · the why · the disclosure line · what to do when unsure ("ask me — asking is never a violation").

**B. The guardrail starter (student-facing, RADAR-style rails)** — matched to continuum level; see the Guardrail Starter library that accompanies this skill.

**C. The process spine** — 2–4 visible artifacts across the arc (proposal, annotated evidence, draft, prompt log, reflection), each low-stakes, each seen by a human.

**D. The disclosure line** — one sentence students complete: "During this work I used [tool] to [do what]." Normalize it as scholarly transparency, not confession.

---

## Step 5: Second pass

Run the rebuilt assignment through all 7 personas again, all 3 layers.

**Pass criteria:**
- Zero CRITICAL failures remaining
- No more than 2 HIGH PRIORITY failures remaining (with documented reasoning for why those tradeoffs are acceptable)

If it fails: repeat steps 4 and 5. The assignment is not approved until it passes.

---

## Output format

### PART 1: MISUSE-PRESSURE REPORT

**Section A — Structural Audit Results.** Each of the 7 audit questions: PASS or FAIL with a one-line explanation.

**Section B — Persona Failure Matrix.** 7 rows (personas) × 3 columns (layers). Each cell: "PASS" or a short failure statement pointing to the exact moment the assignment breaks.

**Section C — Prioritized Failure List.** CRITICAL / HIGH / LOW, clustered by where they hit.

### PART 2: REBUILT ASSIGNMENT

- **Header:** kernel of cognition · continuum level · what changed and why (one short paragraph)
- **The rebuilt assignment brief**, student-facing, including the AI expectations block, guardrail language with its why, process spine, and disclosure line
- **Second-pass results:** pass/fail; document any accepted tradeoffs

---

## A note on tone

Most good assignments invite two to four of the eleven reasons without anyone intending it. Finding failures is design, not blame — and good teaching is still the best integrity builder.
